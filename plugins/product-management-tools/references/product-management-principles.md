# Product Management Principles

Use these principles as shared product management context across the skills.
Apply only the parts that are relevant to the user's artifact and decision.

## Core Product Management Model

Product management connects customer problems, business outcomes, feasible
solutions, and go-to-market execution. Good product work makes tradeoffs
explicit: who benefits, what changes, why now, what will not be done, and how
success will be judged.

Prefer outcome language over output language. A feature is only useful when it
changes customer behavior, business performance, operational quality, risk, or
learning speed.

## Discovery

Discovery should reduce the biggest uncertainty before delivery investment.
Use customer evidence, behavioral data, support signals, sales feedback,
competitive pressure, operational constraints, and technical reality.

Separate:

- Problem: the pain, need, job, opportunity, or risk.
- Customer segment: who experiences it and under what conditions.
- Evidence: what proves the problem matters.
- Desired outcome: what will be better if the work succeeds.
- Solution idea: one possible way to create the outcome.

Do not let a stakeholder request become a requirement until the underlying
problem, owner, priority, and success measure are clear.

## Delivery

Delivery artifacts should help design, engineering, QA, data, GTM, support, and
leadership make aligned decisions. They should define scope boundaries,
acceptance criteria, dependencies, risks, rollout approach, and instrumentation.

Good delivery plans preserve optionality where uncertainty is high and create
commitment where confidence is high.

## Metrics

Use metrics to make decisions, not only to decorate documents. Prefer a small
set of measures that can answer whether the product change worked.

Common metric types:

- Adoption: who uses it and how often.
- Activation: whether users reach the first meaningful value.
- Engagement: repeated valuable use.
- Retention: whether value persists over time.
- Revenue or cost: business impact.
- Quality: reliability, latency, errors, defects, support load.
- Risk: compliance, abuse, security, privacy, operational exposure.

Include baseline, target, measurement window, owner, and instrumentation gaps
when possible.

## Prioritization

Prioritization is a decision about sequencing scarce capacity. Make criteria
visible and avoid false precision.

Common criteria:

- Impact: expected customer or business value.
- Confidence: strength of evidence.
- Effort: design, engineering, data, QA, GTM, and operational cost.
- Urgency: time sensitivity or cost of delay.
- Risk reduction: how much uncertainty or exposure is removed.
- Strategic fit: contribution to current themes and goals.
- Dependency: whether the work unlocks or blocks other work.

Use RICE for comparable opportunities, WSJF for cost-of-delay conversations,
and MoSCoW for release-scope negotiation. Adjust ranking for dependencies and
capacity reality.

## Roadmaps

Roadmaps are communication and decision tools, not feature calendars. They
should explain the strategy, sequencing logic, confidence level, and decision
points.

Prefer themes, outcomes, bets, and time horizons over exact dates unless the
organization has a real delivery constraint. Mark what is committed, planned,
exploratory, or dependent on learning.

## Common Failure Modes

- Starting from a solution without validating the problem.
- Confusing stakeholder urgency with customer or business value.
- Writing goals that describe shipping instead of changed outcomes.
- Hiding non-goals, constraints, or unresolved decisions.
- Using metrics that cannot change a decision.
- Treating estimates as commitments before uncertainty is reduced.
- Ranking work without acknowledging dependencies or capacity.
- Publishing a roadmap that different audiences interpret differently.
