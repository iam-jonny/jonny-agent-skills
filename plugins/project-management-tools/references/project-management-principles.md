# Project Management Principles

Use these principles as shared project management context across the skills.
Apply only the parts that are relevant to the user's project, artifact, and
decision.

## Core Project Management Model

Project management creates reliable progress under constraints. Good project
work clarifies outcomes, scope, owners, milestones, dependencies, risks,
decision rights, communication cadence, and change control.

A project artifact should help the team answer:

- What outcome are we trying to create?
- What is in scope and out of scope?
- Who owns decisions and delivery?
- What milestones matter?
- What dependencies or risks could change the plan?
- How will stakeholders know whether the project is on track?
- What happens when scope, timing, cost, or quality changes?

## Intake Synthesis

Raw project inputs are usually mixed: meeting notes, stakeholder comments,
action items, risks, decisions, assumptions, constraints, and solution ideas.
Synthesis should separate those signals before turning them into a plan.

Separate:

- Confirmed facts.
- Assumptions.
- Decisions made.
- Decisions needed.
- Goals and success criteria.
- Scope candidates.
- Risks and issues.
- Dependencies.
- Action items with owners and dates.
- Stakeholder concerns.

Do not convert every meeting comment into scope. Preserve uncertainty and
attribution when it affects ownership or approval.

## Planning

A project plan should connect scope, schedule, ownership, dependencies, risks,
and communication. Plans should be specific enough to coordinate execution but
honest about uncertainty.

Good plans distinguish:

- Milestones: meaningful delivery or decision points.
- Tasks: work needed to reach milestones.
- Dependencies: work or decisions that block progress.
- Assumptions: conditions the plan depends on.
- Risks: uncertain events that could affect outcomes.
- Issues: current problems already affecting the project.

## Risk and Issue Management

Risk management is not a list-making exercise. It is a way to decide where to
reduce uncertainty, prepare contingencies, or escalate.

For each risk, capture:

- Risk statement.
- Impact.
- Probability or confidence.
- Trigger.
- Owner.
- Mitigation.
- Contingency.
- Escalation threshold.

Issues require action, owner, and due date. Risks require monitoring and
response planning.

## Stakeholder Communication

Stakeholder communication should reduce surprise. Different stakeholders need
different levels of detail, timing, and decision rights.

Useful stakeholder mapping separates:

- Sponsor.
- Decision owner.
- Approvers.
- Contributors.
- Consulted parties.
- Informed parties.
- Potential blockers.

Communication should make status, decisions, asks, risks, and next steps clear.

## Status Reporting

A useful status report is a decision tool, not a diary. It should show whether
the project is on track, what changed, what needs attention, and what decisions
or help are required.

Prefer:

- RAG status with explanation.
- Progress against milestones.
- Key accomplishments.
- Upcoming work.
- Risks and issues.
- Decisions needed.
- Scope, schedule, cost, or quality changes.
- Help requested.

## Change Control

Change requests should be evaluated against scope, schedule, cost, quality,
risk, stakeholder value, and strategic fit. A change is not approved because it
is useful; it is approved when the tradeoff is explicit and accepted by the
right decision owner.

Capture:

- Requested change.
- Reason.
- Business or project value.
- Impact on scope, timeline, cost, quality, risk, and dependencies.
- Alternatives.
- Recommendation.
- Decision owner.
- Approval conditions.

## Retrospectives

Retrospectives should produce learning that changes future behavior. Separate
facts, observations, root causes, decisions, and follow-up actions.

Useful retro outputs include:

- What went well.
- What did not go well.
- Root causes.
- Patterns.
- Action items.
- Owners.
- Follow-up date.

Avoid vague lessons. A good retro action changes a process, decision rule,
handoff, checklist, template, or communication pattern.

## Common Failure Modes

- Starting delivery before outcomes and scope are clear.
- Treating a deadline as a plan.
- Listing tasks without dependencies, owners, or decision points.
- Hiding assumptions until they fail.
- Reporting green status while unmanaged risks are growing.
- Treating every stakeholder request as approved scope.
- Confusing risks with issues.
- Approving changes without tradeoffs.
- Holding retrospectives that produce observations but no behavior change.
